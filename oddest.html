OddestSea

Project began in August 2018. Game first launched on itch.io March 1, 2020 and is available for download here. Our Steam page can be viewed here.

My contributions to OddestSea:

  • Collaborated with designers to create monster and environmental interactions
    • Early monster behaviors were too ambitious and I worked with the development team to re-evaluate monster designs by considering what was possible given player feedback and team resources
    • Iterated upon shark behavior to create simple, choreographed movement and attacks that players could consciously predict
    • Created wind, seaweed, and buoy interactions to enhance/hinder sailing and provide short-term goals
    • Gave players greater control and more choices when encountering monsters or other obstacles

  • Worked in other areas as needed

    • Provided critical feedback to artists and other designers to optimize visual aesthetic and gameplay
    • Helped team members debug problems in engine and discuss possible causes and solutions
    • Organized design documentation which can be viewed here

  • Helped the team overcome problems we had with iterating on player feedback and cutting content
    • Sailing was not fun, controls were confusing
    • Not enough points of interest or choices to keep player attention for very long
    • Combat system was a constant problem, felt clunky, extraneous, and distracted from the strong visuals
    • Team had trouble figuring out what was feasible given available resources
    • Process:
      • Evaluated available time and other tasks to determine what was out of scope
      • Engaged in group decision making with producer, design, and programming teams at weekly meetings to discuss content cuts
      • Referred back to our core vision to cut systems that did not fit (combat)
      • Collaborated on level design and moved away from wide-open map to obstacle course layout with the final goal at the center (lighthouse)
      • Thoroughly described problems with boat movement that both designers and programmers could understand and act on
    • Results:
      • Better represented the experience of 'being pursued' we wanted to accomplish
      • Player was not completely lost sailing without any direction
      • Put greater emphasis on our strong visual aesthetic
      • More enjoyable and smooth boat movement/sailing
      • Reduced overall scope
